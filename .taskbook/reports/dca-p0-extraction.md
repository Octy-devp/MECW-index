## P0-1: MECW11-004 — The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte (Marx, 1852)

### Crisis（危機診斷）

```yaml
crisis_diagnosis:
  type: revolutionary_defeat_and_its_counterrevolutionary_outcome
  specific: |
    Louis Bonaparte's coup d'état of December 2, 1851. The crisis Marx
    diagnoses is not the coup itself but WHY the February Revolution of
    1848 — which proclaimed the 'social republic' — ended with a
    farcical imitation of Napoleon I by his nephew. How did the most
    advanced class struggle in European history produce BONAPARTISM?
    
    Marx's answer is structural: the French bourgeoisie, terrified by
    the June 1848 workers' insurrection, chose to sacrifice its own
    political rule (parliamentary republic) for the security of its
    social power (property). Bonapartism is the form of state in which
    the bourgeoisie RULES but does not GOVERN — it delegates political
    power to a dictator to protect its economic interests.
    
  evidence: |
    "The bourgeoisie... confessed that its own interest demanded its
    rescue from the danger of its own rule; that... in order to
    preserve its social power intact, its political power must be
    broken; that the individual bourgeois can continue to exploit
    the other classes and enjoy property, family, religion and order
    only on condition that his class be condemned to the same
    political nullity as the other classes."
    
lag_mechanism:
  layers: [class_fragmentation, ideological_inheritance, state_apparatus]
  
  class_fragmentation: |
    The French peasantry — the largest class — cannot represent itself.
    'They cannot represent themselves; they must be represented.'
    Parcelized production isolates peasants from each other; they form
    a class 'insofar as they live in economic conditions that separate
    their mode of life... from those of other classes' but NOT a class
    'insofar as there is merely a local interconnection among these
    small-holding peasants.' This structural isolation makes them the
    social base of Bonapartism — they see in Louis Bonaparte the
    restoration of the Napoleonic glory that gave them their land.
    
  ideological_inheritance: |
    'The tradition of all dead generations weighs like a nightmare on
    the brains of the living.' The actors of 1848-51 dressed in the
    costumes of 1789 and 1799 — but the content was entirely different.
    The lag is that revolutionary consciousness remains trapped in the
    forms of past revolutions, unable to create its own language.
    The proletarian revolution, by contrast, 'cannot draw its poetry
    from the past but only from the future.'
    
  state_apparatus: |
    Bonapartism is not just a person but a STATE FORM. The executive
    power, with its 'enormous bureaucratic and military organization,'
    has made itself independent of society. Marx traces the growth of
    this parasitic state machine from absolute monarchy through the
    First Republic (which perfected it) to Napoleon I and now Louis
    Bonaparte. The lag is that NO previous revolution dismantled this
    apparatus — each merely transferred it to new hands. The lesson
    for the proletariat: 'All revolutions perfected this machine
    instead of smashing it.'
    
alternative_proposal:
  method: historical_materialist_analysis_as_political_strategy
  elements:
    - The key strategic insight: the proletariat must SMASH the
      existing state machine, not capture it
    - Alliance with the peasantry: show small-holding peasants that
      their interests coincide with the urban proletariat because
      'the small holding... has become the pretext for the
      expropriation of the agricultural producer by the capitalist'
    - The proletariat as the ONLY consistently revolutionary class —
      all others (petty bourgeoisie, peasantry) oscillate and
      vacillate. The workers' party must lead, not tail
    - The form of analysis IS the form of intervention: Marx does not
      write a political program; he writes a historical analysis that
      reveals the necessity of proletarian revolution
      
direction_constraint:
  core: class_structure_determines_political_forms
  specific: |
    'Men make their own history, but they do not make it just as
    they please; they do not make it under circumstances chosen by
    themselves, but under circumstances directly encountered, given
    and transmitted from the past.'
    
    The material constraints Marx identifies:
    1. The French class structure (fragmented peasantry, terrified
       bourgeoisie, defeated proletariat) makes Bonapartism possible
    2. The state apparatus accumulated over centuries cannot be
       'reformed' — it must be SMASHED (this is the first explicit
       formulation of this principle in Marx's work)
    3. The proletarian revolution requires its OWN political forms,
       not the recycling of bourgeois revolutionary traditions
    4. Economic development under Bonapartism creates the conditions
       for its own overthrow: the very modernization Louis Bonaparte
       promotes will strengthen the proletariat
```

## P0-2: MECW24-035 — Socialism: Utopian and Scientific (Engels, 1880)

### Crisis（危機診断）

```yaml
crisis_diagnosis:
  type: theoretical_disarmament_of_the_workers_movement
  specific: |
    In 1880, the French workers' movement is reviving after the
    massacre of the Commune. But its theoretical level is dangerously
    low. French socialism is dominated by Proudhonist mutualism,
    Blanquist putschism, and eclectic 'socialism' that mixes Marx
    with Lassalle with utopian schemes. Engels' intervention is
    specifically aimed at the French Workers' Party (Parti Ouvrier),
    founded in 1879 with Jules Guesde and Paul Lafargue.
    
    The crisis: a mass workers' party exists but lacks THEORETICAL
    CLARITY about what distinguishes scientific socialism from the
    various utopian and eclectic currents that claim the name
    'socialist.' Without this clarity, the party will be pulled
    back into bourgeois radicalism.
    
    This text is Engels' most successful popularization — not a
    simplification but a CONCENTRATION of the theoretical core,
    extracted from Anti-Dühring at Marx's suggestion.
    
lag_mechanism:
  layers: [intellectual_tradition, class_development, organizational]
  
  intellectual_tradition: |
    The lag Engels traces is the historical development of socialism
    itself. Utopian socialism (Saint-Simon, Fourier, Owen) arose when
    capitalist production was still undeveloped. It had to INVENT
    socialism from the head because the material conditions for it
    did not yet exist. 'To the crude conditions of capitalist
    production... corresponded crude theories.' The lag is that
    these early forms persist as IDEOLOGICAL RESIDUES within the
    workers' movement long after the material conditions that
    produced them have been superseded.
    
  class_development: |
    The utopians could not see the proletariat as the agent of
    socialist transformation because the proletariat as a class
    was itself still 'undeveloped.' The utopians appealed to
    'society as a whole' — to the ruling class — to implement
    their schemes. This was not a moral failing but a historical
    limitation: 'the class struggle was as yet only in its
    beginnings.'
    
  organizational: |
    The French workers' party is in formation. Without theoretical
    clarity, it risks becoming a sect — or worse, a tail of bourgeois
    radicalism. Engels' pamphlet is an ORGANIZING intervention: it
    provides the theoretical foundation that enables the party to
    distinguish itself from both utopian sects and bourgeois reformers.
    
alternative_proposal:
  method: historical_materialist_genealogy_of_socialism_itself
  elements:
    - Two great discoveries make socialism scientific:
      1. The materialist conception of history: 'the final causes
         of all social changes... are to be sought not in men's
         brains, not in their better insight into eternal truth
         and justice, but in changes in the mode of production
         and exchange'
      2. The theory of surplus value: 'the secret of capitalist
         production' — revealing exploitation not as a moral
         failing but as the structural mechanism of the system
    - The contradiction specific to capitalism: SOCIALIZED production
      vs CAPITALIST appropriation. This is the source of periodic
      crises, the concentration of capital, and the growth of the
      proletariat
    - The state as 'the ideal personification of the total national
      capital' — the capitalist class organized as a public power
    - The proletarian revolution: seizing the means of production
      and transforming them into social property. 'The proletariat
      seizes the public power and by means of this transforms the
      socialized means of production into public property'
    - The result: 'the government of persons is replaced by the
      administration of things' — the state 'withers away'
      
direction_constraint:
  core: productive_forces_create_both_the_necessity_and_possibility_of_socialism
  specific: |
    'Modern socialism is... the product of the recognition... of the
    class antagonisms between capitalists and wage-workers... and of
    the anarchy in production.'
    
    But significantly: 'its theoretical form is... the continuation
    of the most highly developed philosophical traditions' — German
    classical philosophy, French utopian socialism, English political
    economy. Socialism is not a rejection of bourgeois culture but
    its CRITICAL APPROPRIATION.
    
    The material constraint that makes socialism possible:
    'The possibility of securing for every member of society...
    an existence not only fully sufficient from the material
    standpoint... but also guaranteeing the free development
    and exercise of his physical and mental faculties — this
    possibility now exists for the first time, but it EXISTS.'
    The productive forces have developed to the point where
    SOCIALISM IS MATERIALLY POSSIBLE. The obstacle is not
    scarcity but the capitalist form of appropriation.
```

## P0-3: MECW27-004 — Foreign Policy of Russian Tsardom (Engels, 1890)

### Crisis（危機診斷）

```yaml
crisis_diagnosis:
  type: geopolitical_trajectory_toward_european_war
  specific: |
    In 1890, Europe is dividing into two armed camps: the Triple
    Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) and the emerging
    Franco-Russian alliance. Engels, at the end of his life, sees
    a WORLD WAR approaching — and he traces its origins to the
    centuries-long expansion of Russian Tsardom.
    
    This is NOT a general critique of 'imperialism' or 'militarism.'
    It is a specific historical analysis: Russian Tsardom, from
    Peter the Great through Catherine II to Alexander III, has
    pursued a systematic policy of territorial expansion —
    westward into Poland and the Baltic, southward toward
    Constantinople and the Straits, eastward into Central Asia.
    This expansion is not the whim of individual tsars but a
    STRUCTURAL feature of the Russian state form.
    
    The immediate crisis: the 1890 lapse of the Reinsurance Treaty
    between Germany and Russia, and Bismarck's dismissal by
    Wilhelm II, removes the diplomatic brake on Franco-Russian
    alliance. Engels predicts: a general European war, fought
    with unprecedented violence, that will either destroy
    Tsardom or provoke revolution.
    
    KEY INSIGHT: Engels is not a pacifist. He analyzes the class
    content of foreign policy. Russian Tsardom is the 'last great
    fortress of the European reaction' — its defeat is a
    precondition for the victory of the European proletariat.
    
lag_mechanism:
  layers: [geopolitical, class_base, diplomatic]
  
  geopolitical: |
    Russia's expansion is geographically driven but CLASS-MEDIATED.
    'Russian diplomacy... constitutes a power that even the strongest
    tsar must obey.' The Foreign Office has its own institutional
    inertia, independent of any individual ruler. Russia needs
    warm-water ports (Black Sea, Baltic) and control of the Straits
    to become a genuine world power.
    
  class_base: |
    Tsardom rests on a specific class alliance: the nobility +
    the nascent bourgeoisie, unified by territorial expansion as
    a substitute for internal reform. 'The Russian peasants, ground
    down by taxes and redemption payments, are the material basis
    of Tsardom's foreign adventures.' Expansion abroad compensates
    for the impossibility of reform at home.
    
  diplomatic: |
    Western European powers (especially Britain and France) have
    consistently underestimated or collaborated with Russian
    expansion. Engels traces a century of diplomatic blindness:
    'The whole of Europe has always believed in Russia's moderation.'
    This ideological lag — the liberal fantasy that Tsardom can be
    appeased — enables its continued expansion.
    
alternative_proposal:
  method: class_analysis_of_geopolitics_as_strategic_orientation
  elements:
    - The defeat of Russian Tsardom is a revolutionary necessity.
      Engels is explicit: the overthrow of the Tsar by internal
      revolution would 'remove the mainstay of all reaction in
      Europe' and 'give a fresh impulse to the working-class
      movement of the whole continent'
    - European workers' parties must oppose THEIR OWN ruling classes'
      alliance with Tsardom. For German social democracy, this means
      opposing Wilhelm II's Russia policy. For the French, opposing
      the Franco-Russian alliance that serves Tsarist interests
    - The strategic orientation: a RUSSIAN REVOLUTION would be the
      spark for European-wide socialist transformation. Engels sees
      Russia as 'the vanguard of revolutionary action in Europe' —
      NOT because of its advanced capitalism (it's backward) but
      because its state form (Tsardom) is the most concentrated
      expression of reaction
    - This is an early articulation of what later becomes the
      theory of the 'weakest link' — revolution may break out
      first where the contradictions are most acute, not where
      capitalism is most developed
      
direction_constraint:
  core: tsarist_state_form_enables_and_constrains_geopolitical_strategy
  specific: |
    Engels is not calling for a crusade against Russia. He analyzes
    the MATERIAL basis of Tsarist expansion and the conditions under
    which it can be defeated:
    
    1. Tsardom's military might rests on a backward peasant economy —
       'Russia is as yet hardly a capitalist country.' A modern war
       would strain this base to breaking point
    2. The specific form of the Russian state — autocratic, based on
       territorial expansion as legitimation — makes it VULNERABLE
       to military defeat: 'One good drubbing... would work wonders'
    3. The narodniks and early Russian Marxists are already
       organizing. Engels sees the revolutionary potential in Russia
       despite (or because of) its backwardness
    4. The DIRECTION is NOT toward a Russian-led 'liberation' of
       Europe but toward the NEUTRALIZATION of Tsardom as a
       reactionary power — which would free the European proletariat
       to pursue its own revolution
```
